LLM Definition

LLM stands for Large Language Model.

An LLM is an artificial intelligence model trained on massive amounts of text data so it can:

  1. Understand language
  2. Generate responses
  3. Reason over information
  4. Perform tasks like Q&A, summarization, translation, etc.

Context Window

In LLM, there is a context window, which is the maximum number of tokens that you can provide to it at one time.

Example: "The Cat sat on the mat"

Let us assume, we have 6 tokens in the above sentence

GPT-4o can process 128K tokens at a time

Cons / Limitations of LLMs

1. Limited to training data timeframe

An LLM only knows information up to the time it was trained. If you ask about events or updates after its training cutoff, it may:

  • Knowledge cutoff: It only knows information up to its last training date
  • Guess (hallucinate)
  • Or say it doesn't know

2. LLMs can hallucinate

LLMs sometimes produce answers that sound correct but are factually wrong because they rely only on patterns learned during training.

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) â€“ Definition

RAG is an AI technique that combines a Large Language Model (LLM) with an external knowledge retrieval system to produce accurate, up-to-date, and domain-specific answers.

In simple terms:

RAG retrieves relevant information from your documents or database, and then the LLM uses that information to generate the final answer.

How RAG solves this?

RAG retrieves relevant, domain-specific, up-to-date information from your document base, it injects the retrieved content into the LLM as context, so the model answers based on real data, not guesses (no Hallucinations).

RAG retrieves real, up-to-date, domain-specific documents (manuals, SOPs, logs, etc.) and provides them to the LLM.
